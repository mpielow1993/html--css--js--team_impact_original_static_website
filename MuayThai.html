Muay Thai

What is Muay Thai?

Muay Thai is a hard martial art from Thailand and is the country's national sport. Muay Thai as it is practiced today varies significantly from its ancestor Muay Boran. Similar in format to Western Boxing, in modern Muay Thai two opponents fight each other under the supervision of a referee over 3-5 rounds of between 2 and 3 minutes each. They can win via Knockout, Technical Knock Out or, should the contest last the full duration, a judges' decision.

Muay Thai is referred to as the ‘Art of Eight Limbs’ because it makes use of punches, kicks, elbows and knee strikes, thus using eight ‘points of contact’, as opposed to ‘two points’ (fists) in Western Boxing and ‘four points’ (hands and feet) used in sport-oriented martial arts.

A large part of Muay Thai, and also what makes it useful for MMA, is the standing Thai clinch (often just referred to as ‘the clinch’). In contrast to Western Boxing, where two fighters are separated if they end up in a clinch situation, in Muay Thai the fight keeps going. There are many techniques from this position that a proficient Nak Muay (Thai fighter) will use to control their opponent and execute sweeps and devastating elbow and knee strikes.

What is Muay Thai training like?

Training sessions typically involve some form of high intensity conditioning specific to Muay Thai such as


  • Skipping rope

  • Heavy bag work

  • Thai Pad work

  • Clinch drilling

All classes are appropriately supervised by our Muay Thai instructor Aaron Jennings. Proper technique is stressed at all times. This will be taught by the instructor, then an individual will drill the taught technique with a progressively resisting partner.


With the permission of the instructor, students who are deemed technically proficient and responsible enough will be allowed to attend the intermediate and advanced classes and spar.
